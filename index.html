A lesson from physics

The Luminiferous Aether

Aristotle
C. Huygens

Sound needs air. Ocean waves need water.
So light must need… something.

In 1690, Christiaan Huygens formalized the aether as the medium for light waves. For over a century after, every equation in optics assumed it. Every physicist believed it.

Never observed. Never measured. But it had to exist — because the math didn't work without it.

1887

Michelson–Morley

A. Michelson
E. Morley

They built the most precise interferometer ever made. Split a beam in two, sent the halves perpendicular, looked for a speed difference.

There was no difference.

The most famous null result in physics.

Expected fringe shift vs. observed — null result

A. A. Michelson, E. W. Morley. "On the Relative Motion of the Earth and the Luminiferous Ether", American Journal of Science, 1887.

"What if we just… don't need it?"
The parallel

Deep learning has
its own aether

In a Transformer, attention is the one component with a clear mathematical framework for interpretability — we can decompose it into circuits and trace information flow.

The MLP block is different. Two linear projections sandwiching an activation function ($\sigma$). The linear parts are interpretable — but $\sigma$ breaks the chain. It has no closed-form inverse, no geometric meaning, no mathematical structure to decompose.

And this repeats every layer. The distortion compounds.

Transformer block — teal = explainable, red = black box

The deeper you go

It only gets worse

Each layer passes through another activation. The distortions compound. By layer 64, the original signal is unrecoverable.

Even Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) — the best tool mechanistic interpretability has — explicitly state they cannot mathematically explain what these layers compute.

Statistical correlations, not causal structure. The activations have no mathematical structure to probe.

The root cause

Manifold distortion

ReLU maps all negatives to zero. Vectors that were opposite (180° apart — maximally different meaning) become orthogonal (0° — semantically unrelated). The metric structure of the data manifold is non-recoverable.

Left: 20 points on a unit circle — opposite pairs connected. Right: after ReLU — 3 quadrants collapse to axes/origin.

A clue from nature

The inverse-square law

When physicists removed the aether, what remained were the actual laws. Gravity, electrostatics, radiation — the universe is overwhelmingly governed by one pattern:

$$F \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$$

Influence decays with the square of distance. Locality is the physics — not a heuristic, a law.

What if neural interactions obeyed this same law? What if the denominator of a neural operation encoded proximity, not just the numerator encoding alignment?

Inverse-square potential — masses partition space by proximity, not magnitude

The insight

What if we go back to Mercer?

In standard neural networks, input and weight spaces are linked by the dot product — a linear, flat metric. But Mercer's theorem (1909) showed there's a richer structure:

$$K(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \langle \varphi(\mathbf{x}),\; \varphi(\mathbf{y}) \rangle_{\mathcal{H}}$$

Every continuous positive-definite kernel implicitly maps data into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space where geometry — distances, angles, curvature — is defined by the kernel itself.

Input space → kernel map $\varphi$ → RKHS where geometry is meaningful

J. Mercer. "Functions of Positive and Negative Type, and their Connection with the Theory of Integral Equations", Phil. Trans. Royal Society A, 1909.
Core contribution

The Yat-Product ⵟ

$$\mathcal{K}_{\text{ⵟ}}(\mathbf{w},\mathbf{x}) = \frac{(\mathbf{w}^{\top}\mathbf{x})^{2}}{\|\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{x}\|^{2}+\varepsilon}$$

Numerator — Alignment

$(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x})^2$

Directional agreement.

Denominator — Proximity

$\|\mathbf{w} - \mathbf{x}\|^2 + \varepsilon$

Built-in normalization.

Mercer kernel Analytic Lipschitz Self-regularizing

ⵟ-kernel potential well with level sets — $\mathbf{w}$ fixed at center

T. Bouhsine. "No More DeLuLu: Physics-Inspired Kernel Networks for Geometrically-Grounded Neural Computation", arXiv:2603.12276, 2025.

Beyond interpretability

What kernel theory also brings

A principled kernel isn't just about understanding — it unlocks linear-time approximation.

Random Feature Approximation

$K(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) \approx \Psi(\mathbf{x})^\top \Psi(\mathbf{y})$

Any shift-invariant kernel can be approximated by finite-dimensional random features (Rahimi & Recht, 2007). This turns $O(n^2)$ kernel operations into $O(n \cdot m)$ — linear in sequence length.

Bernstein Decomposition

$\text{ⵟ}_{\text{sph}} = \int_0^\infty e^{-sC} x^2 e^{2sx}\,ds$

The ⵟ-kernel's complete monotonicity enables exact decomposition via Gauss-Laguerre quadrature — not an approximation, but a mathematical identity.

This is how SLAY achieves $O(n)$ attention: the kernel trick, applied rigorously.

A. Rahimi, B. Recht. "Random Features for Large-Scale Kernel Machines", NeurIPS, 2007.  |  J. M. Luna, T. Bouhsine, K. Choromanski. "SLAY", arXiv:2602.04915, 2025.
Applying the kernel to attention

SLAY

Spherical Linearized Attention with Yat-Kernel

Constrain Q, K to the unit sphere. Replace softmax with the ⵟ-kernel. Linearize via Bernstein decomposition.

$$\hat{\mathbf{Y}}=\frac{\Psi({\mathbf{Q}})\left(\Psi({\mathbf{K}})^{\top}\mathbf{V}\right)}{\Psi({\mathbf{Q}})\left(\Psi({\mathbf{K}})^{\top}\mathbf{1}\right)}$$

$O(n)$ attention — interpretable, geometry-aware, linear in sequence length.

The reality

You can't compete with big tech
on compute

GPUs are expensive. Random experiments to validate a hypothesis would cost a small nation's GDP. If you're an indie researcher from the outskirts of Morocco, every GPU hour counts.

You need to minimize cost. You can't brute-force your way to discovery. Every experiment must be intentional.

What you need

Constraints shape the approach

↻

Reproducibility

You need to know you're not winning the lottery ticket. Full control over randomness, deterministic results, or your experiments mean nothing.

⚙

No CUDA hell

You don't want to spend months writing custom CUDA kernels for the sake of testing a mathematical hypothesis. The tool must match the math.

⚡

Hardware access

You need accelerators you can actually afford. H100 clusters are not an option.

JAX & Flax

JAX thinks like a mathematician. Functional transforms that mirror the math — no framework tax, no abstraction gap.

The hardest part

The proofs, not the experiments

The hardest part of this research wasn't compute. It wasn't engineering. It was the months spent at the whiteboard proving the theorems.

Without proofs

Random experiments. Hoping for a good loss curve. No guarantee your result generalizes. Burning compute on intuition.

With proofs

The theorem tells you what should work and why. Experiments become validation, not exploration. Every run is guided by mathematics.

Once we proved the ⵟ-kernel was a valid Mercer kernel — analytic, Lipschitz, self-regularizing — the experiments followed directly. The math guided us to what to test.
